TMJ (Jaw Joint) Surgeries

Relief from Chronic Jaw Pain

The Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) connects your jawbone to your skull, and is responsible for opening, closing, and moving your jaw. TMJ disorders (TMD) are a group of painful conditions affecting this joint and the surrounding muscles. Symptoms can include persistent jaw pain, headaches, earaches, clicking or popping sounds, and difficulty opening or closing the mouth (locking).

While many cases can be managed with non-surgical treatments like physical therapy or bite guards, some patients with severe, chronic, or degenerative problems may require surgery. The ultimate goal of surgery is to relieve pain, restore normal jaw function, and significantly improve the patient's quality of life when conservative options have failed.

Types of TMJ Surgery

The type of surgery recommended depends entirely on the specific diagnosis, moving from the least invasive to the most comprehensive options.

Arthrocentesis & Arthroscopy (Minimally Invasive)

Arthrocentesis is a simple procedure to flush out inflammatory substances and relieve pressure within the joint. TMJ arthroscopy uses a tiny camera (arthroscope) inserted into the joint through a small incision. This "keyhole surgery" allows the surgeon to visualize the joint, remove inflamed tissue, and reposition a displaced disc with minimal scarring and faster recovery.

Open-Joint Surgery (Arthroplasty)

For more advanced structural problems, an open-joint procedure may be necessary. This involves a carefully placed incision to directly access and repair or remove damaged tissue. This may be done to treat bony growths (osteophytes), significant scarring (adhesions), or to repair or remove the articular disc.

Total Joint Replacement

In cases of severe degeneration from conditions like osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or after severe trauma, the damaged joint can be replaced with a custom-made artificial prosthesis. This procedure is highly effective for restoring function and eliminating pain in patients with the most advanced, end-stage TMJ disease.
