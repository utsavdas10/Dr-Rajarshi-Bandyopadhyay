Microvascular Reconstructive Surgery

Rebuilding Form and Restoring Function

Reconstructive surgery aims to restore form and function to parts of the body affected by trauma, cancer, or congenital defects. In the head and neck, this is a highly complex field that requires not only exceptional surgical skill but also an artistic eye for aesthetics. Dr. Bandyopadhyay specializes in advanced reconstructive techniques, particularly microvascular free flap surgery, which is the gold standard for rebuilding complex defects of the jaw, tongue, and face.

The ultimate goal is not just to close a wound, but to reconstruct the anatomy in a way that allows a person to speak, eat, breathe, and appear as normal as possible, thereby maximizing their quality of life after a major life event.

The Reconstructive Process

Reconstruction is almost always performed at the same time as the primary surgery (e.g., tumor removal or trauma repair) to provide the best possible outcome in a single, comprehensive operation.

Step 1: The Two-Team Approach

Major reconstructive surgeries are performed with two surgical teams working simultaneously. One team focuses on addressing the primary problem (e.g., removing a tumor). At the same time, the second team carefully harvests the "free flap"—the donor tissue with its artery and vein—from the leg, arm, or back. This teamwork significantly reduces the total operating time for the patient.

Step 2: Microvascular Anastomosis and Insetting

Once the defect is ready, the harvested flap is transferred. The surgeon, working under a high-powered operating microscope, painstakingly connects the tiny (1-3mm) artery and veins of the flap to recipient blood vessels in the neck. This "re-plumbing," known as microvascular anastomosis, restores blood flow to the new tissue. The flap is then shaped and secured ("inset") to perfectly reconstruct the defect.
