Benign Tumors & Cysts of the Jaws

Preserving Jaw Structure & Function

Cysts (fluid-filled sacs) and tumors (solid masses of tissue) can develop in the jawbones or soft tissues of the mouth. While the term "tumor" can be alarming, many jaw tumors and most cysts are benign (non-cancerous). However, even benign growths require expert management as they can grow slowly and cause significant problems by displacing teeth, weakening the jawbone, and leading to facial swelling or numbness.

Dr. Bandyopadhyay specializes in the surgical management of these conditions. The goals are always to completely remove the lesion, prevent its recurrence, and preserve the structure and function of the jaw and teeth to the greatest extent possible.

The Surgical Approach

The surgical technique used depends on the size, location, and specific type of the lesion, which is determined by a thorough examination, imaging (like X-rays or CT scans), and often a pre-operative biopsy.

Enucleation and Curettage

This is the most common approach for cysts and well-defined benign tumors. "Enucleation" involves removing the lesion whole, like "shelling out" a pea from its pod. This is often followed by "curettage," which is the careful scraping of the bony cavity to remove any lingering cells and minimize the chance of recurrence. The resulting cavity often heals by filling with new bone on its own, but sometimes a bone graft is placed to accelerate and guide this process.

Resection

For larger or more aggressive benign tumors (such as an ameloblastoma), a more extensive surgery called resection may be required. This involves removing the tumor along with a margin of healthy surrounding bone to ensure complete elimination. This creates a larger defect in the jaw that almost always requires immediate reconstruction with a bone graft from the hip or a microvascular free flap.
